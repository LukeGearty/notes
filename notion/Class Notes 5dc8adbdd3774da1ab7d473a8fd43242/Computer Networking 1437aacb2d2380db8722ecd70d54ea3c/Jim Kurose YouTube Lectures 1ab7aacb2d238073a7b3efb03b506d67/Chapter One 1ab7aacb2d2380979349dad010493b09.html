What is the Internet?

An engineered artifact

A Nuts and Bolts View:

At the edge of the network, there are billions of connected computing devices called hosts/end systems

These hosts are running network apps at the Internet’s “edge”

Moving towards the center, we find Packet Switches - they forward packets - routers, switches

And then there are communication links - fiber, copper, radio, satellite, that have a transmission rate called bandwidth

Networks - collection of devices, routers, links: managed by an organization

These networks give rise to the saying “network of networks” - interconnected ISPs.

The communication is governed by protocols that control sending, receiving of messages

They define a standard way of doing things - the IETF govern the standards, and those standards are known as RFCs

Services POV:

The internet is an infrastructure that provides services to applications: Web, streaming video, multimedia teleconferencing, email, games, e-commerce, social media, interconnected appliances…etc

Provides programming interface to distributed applications

A protocol is a defined way of hosts talking to each other . There are rules for specific messages sent, specific actions taken when message received, or other events. They define the format, order of messages sent and received and actions taken on receipt.

The Network Core

The Network core is the mesh of interconnected routers that are connected via communication links

It is based on packet-switching: The end hosts break application-layers into chunks of data and put them into packets, and send them into the internet. The network forwards packets from one router to the next, across links on path from source to destination

Forwarding and Routing are the two key network-core functions

Forwarding - moving an incoming packet from input link to output link. A local action that utilizes the forwarding table.

Routing: global action - determines source-destination paths taken by packets. A routing algorithm plots the path.

Packet-Switching

Packet transmission delay: takes L / R seconds to transmit L-bit packet into link at R bps

Store and forward: entire packet must arrive at router before it can be transmitted on next link

Queuing: If packets arrive faster than a router can transmit, than a queue forms as packets wait to be forwarded. Packet loss can happen if the queue is too large (larger than queue buffer)

Circuit Switching

End-to-end resources are allocated to, reserved for “call” between source and destination

There is dedicated resources not shared between users - no delays, no loss.

The resources are used for the exclusive use of the call, but if the call goes idle, then the circuit-segment resources are lost - no other calls can use it.

This is done by FDM or TDM.

FDM - Frequency Division Multiplexing - electromagnetic frequencies are divided into narrow frequency bands. Each call is allocated its own band, can transmit at max rate of that narrow band.

Time Division Multiplexing - time divided into slots - each call is allocated a period of slots, can transmit at maximum rate of wider frequency band only during its time slots

Packet switching versus circuit switching

1 Gb/s link

Each user: 100 Mb/s when active, active 10% of time

How many users can use this network under circuit-switching and packet switching?

Circuit-switching: Can support 10 users at a time

Packet switching: with 35 users, probability > 10 active at same time is less than .0004

Telephone networks carry data in packet-switching

It is especially great for bursty data - sometimes has data, sometimes not

Resource sharing, simpler, no call setup

But there is excessive congestion possible: packet delay and loss due to buffer overflow

protocols needed for reliable data transfer, congestion control

Its possible to provide circuit like behavior with packet switching

A “Network of Networks”

How do millions of access ISPs connect together

We can connect them all together - that isn’t feasible as that would be a Big O (n^2)

We can create a global transit ISP where each ISP connects to the global transit network, where one access ISP would connect to another

If one global ISP is a viable business, there will be competitors. There are multiple global ISPs that are interconnected at Internet Exchange Points. Regional ISPs form that connect to the ISPs, and content providers (google, microsoft) run their own networks to bring services, content close to end users.

Performance

Packets queue in router buffers, waiting for turn for transmission

Packet loss occurs when memory to hold queued packets fills up

Processing Delay - nodal processing:

Check bit errors, determine output link. Typically less than microseconds.

Queuing Delay:

time waiting at output link for transmission

depends on congestion level for router

Transmission Delay: L packet length (bits), R: Link transmission rate (bps). Transmission delay is L/R

Propagation Delay:

d - length of physical link

s = propagation speed (~2x10^8 m/sec)

propagation delay = d / s

Packet Queuing Delay

a: average packet arrival rate

L: Packet length (bits)

R: Link bandwidth (bit transmission rate)

L * a = arrival rate of bits

Traffic intensity = (L * a) / R

La/R = ~0: avg queuing delay small

La/R > 1 : avg queuing delay large

Delay is infinite - more work arriving is more than can be serviced

Throughput : the rate in bits/time unit at which bits are being sent from sender to receiver

instantaneous: rate at given point in time

average: rate over longer period of time

Bottleneck Link - the link on end-end path that constrains end-end throughput

Layering, Encapsulation

A complex system - think of airline travel (getting to the airport, gates, baggage claim, different classes, etc). You can think of this as a series of steps.

  1. You get the ticket
  1. You put the baggage
  1. Get the gates
  1. Runway takeoff
  1. airplane takes off
  1. Airplane flies
  1. Airplane lands
  1. Gates (unload)
  1. Get the baggage
  1. You arrive

Why layer this?

Explicit structure allows identification, relationship of system’s pieces

Layered reference model

Modularization eases maintenance, updating of system

Change in layer’s service implementation: transparent to rest of system

Change in gate procedure doesn’t affect rest of system

Layered Internet Protocol Stack

  1. Application: Supporting network applications
    1. HTTP, IMAP, SMTP, DNS
  1. Transport: process-process data transfer
    1. TCP, UDP
  1. Network:routing of datagrams from source to destination
    1. IP, routing, protocols
  1. Link: Data transfer between neighboring network elements
    1. Ethernet, 802.11, PPP
  1. Physical: the “bits on the wire”

Applications exchange “messages” with each other. It sends it to the transport-layer, which adds header to the message to create a segment. Network layer protocol encapsulates transport-layer segment with network-layer header to create a datagram.

As data flows down the stack, headers are added. As they flow up, the headers are acted upon and removed.

Networks Under Attack

Network Security - what can bad actors do, what defenses are designed and deployed?

The internet wasn’t originally designed with security in mind - the original vision was a group of mutually trusting users attached to a transparent network.

Packet Interception - getting copies of packets - packet “sniffing”

IP spoofing - injection of packet with false source address - pretending a packet came from a source it didn’t come from

Denial of Service - attackers make resources unavailable to legitimate traffic by overwhelming resource with bogus traffic

Lines of defense:

  1. Authentication: proving who you are
  1. Confidentiality: via encryption
  1. Integrity checks: digital signatures prevent/detect tampering
  1. access restrictions: password-protected VPNs
  1. Firewalls: specialized “middleboxes” in access and core networks:
    1. off-by-default
    1. detecting DOS attacks
