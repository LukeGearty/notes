Cyber 101

Cyber 101: Information Security

  • Definition: Information Security, or InfoSec, refers to practices, components, and processes used to protect information, especially data containing personally identifiable information (PII).
  • Purpose: Prevents unauthorized access, use, manipulation, and destruction of information. Ensures data remains confidential, accurate, and accessible.

Core Principle: CIA Triad

  1. Confidentiality:
    • Limits access to authorized users only.
  1. Integrity:
    • Ensures data remains accurate and untampered.
  1. Availability:
    • Ensures information and resources are accessible to the right people when needed.

Information Security vs. Cybersecurity

  • Information Security: Broader focus on data protection, including non-digital threats like environmental hazards and physical theft.
  • Cybersecurity: Subset of InfoSec focused on protecting systems, networks, and programs from digital attacks.

Key Concepts in Information Security

  1. Non-repudiation:
    • Guarantees proof of delivery and proof of sender’s identity.
    • Example: Like a signed-for parcel, digital signatures confirm receipt and sender authenticity.
  1. Authentication:
    • Verifies the identity of users and data authenticity.
    • Example: A parcel requiring ID verification upon delivery to ensure it’s genuine.
  1. Accountability:
    • Traces each activity to a specific individual or ID, ensuring proper permissions and roles.
    • Example: In a postal system, each stage of delivery is logged to ensure responsibility can be traced if issues arise.

Security Champions

Security Champions Overview

  • Definition: Security champions are team members who advocate for security awareness and best practices within their teams, acting as a bridge between technical security experts and other employees.
  • Role Expansion: Security champions are no longer exclusive to technical teams. They now appear across departments, including non-technical ones, to promote secure behaviors and answer basic security queries.

Types of Security Champions

  1. Technical Security Champions:
    • Typically embedded in IT or engineering teams.
    • Responsibilities:
      • Amplify security priorities within the team.
      • Highlight security concerns to relevant departments.
      • Assist with code and infrastructure reviews (if skilled).
      • Aid in incident response by collaborating with security teams.
  1. Non-Technical Security Champions:
    • Serve in non-technical teams as primary security advocates.
    • Responsibilities:
      • Act as the first point of contact for security issues.
      • Proactively improve team security practices.
      • Raise awareness and ensure adherence to secure behavior.

Key Responsibilities of All Security Champions

  • Awareness & Training: Encourage security training like phishing simulations and crisis scenarios.
  • Information Sharing: Facilitate the exchange of security information across teams to improve organizational security.
  • Process Review: Stay updated on best practices and assess team processes for potential security issues.
  • Approachability: Champions should be approachable, so colleagues feel comfortable discussing security concerns.

Importance of Security Champions

  • Preparedness: Helps ensure teams are equipped to handle security needs and respond to incidents.
  • Organizational Security Posture: Security champions enhance the company’s security culture, making security a shared responsibility.

Cyber Kill Chain

Cyber Kill Chain Overview

  • Definition: The cyber kill chain is a cybersecurity model that breaks down the steps used by attackers to conduct cyberattacks. It helps security professionals understand attacker strategies, enhance defenses, and inform response actions during an incident.

The Seven Steps of the Cyber Kill Chain

Each step builds upon the previous one, forming a sequential flow required to complete the attack.

  1. Reconnaissance:
    • Attackers gather information about targets and identify possible vulnerabilities or attack methods.
  1. Weaponization:
    • Attack preparation, often involving creating or obtaining malware or exploiting known vulnerabilities.
  1. Delivery:
    • The stage where malware is delivered to the target, usually through methods like phishing emails or malicious downloads.
  1. Exploitation:
    • Attackers exploit vulnerabilities by running malware or code to gain unauthorized access.
  1. Installation:
    • Attackers install tools or software to maintain persistence, such as remote access tools.
  1. Command and Control (C2):
    • Attackers establish control over the compromised system and potentially exfiltrate sensitive data.
  1. Actions on Objectives:
    • Attackers complete their intended goals, which can vary (e.g., data theft, system disruption).

Using the Cyber Kill Chain to Improve Security

  • Identify and Fix Security Gaps: Organizations can review each stage to find and address weaknesses in their defenses.
  • Simulate Cyberattacks: Simulations of realistic attacks (e.g., phishing, social engineering) help pinpoint vulnerabilities and threats.
  • Evaluate Security Controls: Analyze simulations and assign risk scores, creating reports to identify gaps and areas for improvement.
  • Mitigate Security Gaps: Implement fixes, such as patches or additional security tools (e.g., VPNs), to reduce exposure to threats.

Who are the Hackers?

Criminal Hacker

  • Overview: Criminal hackers are financially motivated and are the most common type of hacker affecting everyday users. They target data such as bank details or credit card information for monetary gain.
  • Famous Criminal Hacker: Albert Gonzalez

Nation/State-Sponsored Hacker

  • Overview: State-sponsored hackers are well-funded and supported by government power, focusing on large corporations, other governments, or national infrastructure rather than individuals.
  • State-Sponsored Group: PLA Unit 61398

Hacktivist

  • Overview: Hacktivists hack for political or ideological reasons, targeting entities like corporations or governments with conflicting views.
  • Famous Hacktivist Group: Anonymous Collective

Terrorist Hacker

  • Overview: These hackers may overlap with hacktivist goals but are particularly concerning when attempting cyberterrorist attacks, like targeting national infrastructure (e.g., power grids).
  • Further Reading: More information on cyberterrorism and involved groups is available.

Insider Threat

  • Overview: An insider threat is an employee who attacks their own organization. They are becoming increasingly common.
  • Statistics: In 2021, 57% of organizations reported increased insider threats. Technology, financial services, and healthcare are the most affected industries.
  • Famous Insider Threat: Edward Snowden

Lone Wolf

  • Overview: Lone wolves work independently, making them harder to detect as there are no whistleblowers. Their solitary approach can make them particularly dangerous.
  • Famous Lone Wolf: Jonathan James

Script Kiddie

  • Overview: Script kiddies are inexperienced hackers who rely on scripts written by others. They may cause significant unintentional damage due to a lack of understanding.
  • Example: Unlike experienced hackers who may plan calculated damage for leverage, script kiddies could unintentionally cause severe disruption.
  • Famous Script Kiddie: Gary McKinnon

Ethical Hacker

  • Overview: Ethical hackers operate with permission to identify and fix vulnerabilities in systems, often earning bug bounties from companies for their work.
  • Famous Ethical Hacker: Charlie Miller

Why Hackers Hack

Motivations Behind Hacking

  1. Financial
    • Description: Many hackers, especially criminal groups, are motivated by monetary gain. Techniques include ATM theft, phishing, and selling stolen credit card numbers on the darknet.
    • Objective: Converting cyber skills into financial profit.
  1. Information Theft
    • Description: Common among state-sponsored groups aiming to gather sensitive information to boost national defense capabilities or gain a competitive edge.
    • Example: Reports of Chinese-backed groups targeting defense companies for military technology information.
  1. Political/Ideological
    • Description: Motivated by political beliefs or ideologies, some hackers release damaging information or conduct attacks to influence public opinion or political outcomes.
    • Example: Russian-backed sources allegedly hacked emails of US Democratic Party members in 2016, affecting polling for the Clinton campaign.
  1. Religion
    • Description: Some hacks are religiously motivated, often in retaliation for perceived offenses.
    • Example: In Paris, 19,000 French websites were attacked by extremists following the attack on a satirical newspaper.
  1. Disruption
    • Description: Motivated by revenge, resentment, or the desire to make a statement, these hackers cause service disruptions using methods like DoS (Denial of Service) attacks.
    • Objective: Bringing down servers and disrupting company services temporarily.
  1. The ‘Fun’ Hacker
    • Description: Some hackers are driven by curiosity or enjoyment, exploring vulnerabilities for amusement or to show off. Often, these hackers start with a desire to understand systems or make playful statements.
    • Examples: The rebranding of Burger King’s Twitter to McDonald's, or Rickrolling. A 17-year-old hacked TalkTalk to impress friends.

Cookies

Types of Cookies

  1. Session Cookies (Transient Cookies)
    • Purpose: Temporarily store information about your browsing session.
    • Behavior: Erased when the browser is closed; does not store personal information.
    • Example: Online shopping carts; session cookies help retain items in your cart until checkout. However, session cookies can be a vulnerability if hijacked.
  1. Authentication Cookies
    • Purpose: Verify user login status and allow access to user accounts.
    • Function: Keeps users logged in, so they don’t have to re-enter login credentials repeatedly.
    • Usage: Commonly used by websites requiring account access, like email or social media platforms.
  1. Persistent (Permanent) Cookies
    • Purpose: Stores information long-term; can last months or even years.
    • Function: Recognizes returning users and helps customize website experiences.
    • Alternative Name: Often called "tracking cookies" due to use by advertisers to track browsing habits.
  1. Third-Party Cookies
    • Purpose: Created by external sites, usually for advertising.
    • Function: Tracks user activity across websites for targeted ads.
    • Example: Ads for products you searched on Amazon appearing on unrelated sites.

Cookies and GDPR

  • Privacy Compliance: Cookies are regulated under the EU's GDPR due to privacy concerns. Websites must now show cookie consent banners to inform users about data collection and request permission.

Geolocation Overview

  • Definition: Geolocation determines a device's physical location using latitudinal and longitudinal coordinates. Example uses include tracking fitness activities, tagging locations on social media, and receiving local weather updates.
  • Uses: Helps users find nearby stores, tag social media images, or get location-based information (e.g., local news or events).
  • Risks: Attackers may misuse geolocation data for harassment, stalking, or burglary.

Types of Geolocation

  1. Device-Based Geolocation
    • Method: Uses the device’s GPS and cellular networks for location tracking.
    • Accuracy: High accuracy in populated areas; less accurate in remote regions.
    • Permissions: Apps and websites may request location data access to enhance user experience (e.g., Instagram location tags).
    • Examples: Strava, Sat Nav systems like Google Maps.
  1. Wi-Fi-Based Geolocation (Wi-Fi Positioning System, WPS)
    • Method: Uses Wi-Fi networks for location when GPS is inaccurate or unavailable.
    • Data Collection: Organizations like Google, Apple, and Microsoft collect Wi-Fi network data for this purpose.
    • Example: WiGLE, a database tracking Wi-Fi hotspots worldwide.
  1. Server-Based Geolocation
    • Method: Uses IP addresses from wired/wireless connections to estimate device location.
    • Accuracy: Depends on third-party data mining; generally less precise than GPS.
    • Example: Websites like whatismyipaddress.com.

Privacy and Security Considerations

  • Privacy Risks: Location history may be accessible to apps and websites, allowing detailed tracking of travel routes and frequent locations.
  • Precautions: Users can review location history settings (e.g., on Google Maps) and decide which applications can access location data.
  • VPN Use: VPNs hide IP addresses for server-based geolocation, but device-based geolocation must be disabled manually for full protection.

Fake News Overview

  • Definition: Fake news includes misleading information, hoaxes, or fabricated stories presented as legitimate news. It aims to misinform readers, manipulate opinions, or advance political agendas.
  • Motivations: Often used for profit, as controversial or sensational news attracts attention, boosting readership and revenue for publishers.

Methods of Manipulation

  • Data Breaches: Stolen data is sold online to criminals, who use it to identify biases and target specific audiences.
  • Troll and Botnet Farms: Fake users spread false stories and provoke reactions by tapping into fears and biases.
  • Propaganda: Misinformation can be weaponized to create distrust and division, influencing public sentiment and opinion.

Real vs. Fake News

  • Detection Challenges: Many people find it hard to differentiate between real and fake news, often accepting familiar headlines as true.
  • Impact Example: During the 2016 U.S. election, fake news spread rapidly on social media, potentially impacting voter decisions.
  • Fact-Checking: To verify news credibility, use reputable fact-checking sites like Associated Press, Factcheck.org, and Snopes.

Types of Fake News

  1. Clickbait
    • Description: Uses sensational titles to attract clicks and increase revenue, often with exaggerated or misleading claims.
    • Example: Articles promoting products with fabricated headlines.
  1. Propaganda
    • Description: Content deliberately designed to sway opinions, often used in politically charged topics or government campaigns.
  1. Satire/Parody
    • Description: Humorous content that mocks current events, trends, or figures, usually intended for entertainment rather than deception.
  1. Biased News
    • Description: Articles crafted to appeal to readers’ existing beliefs or biases. Algorithms may reinforce this by showing similar content in social media feeds.

Practical Application

  • Critical Reading: Question the validity of online news and consider its impact on personal views and decisions.
  • Lab Exercise: Identifying real and fake news headlines to build skills in discerning credible sources.

Keylogger Overview

  • Definition: A keylogger is an application or device that records keystrokes on a user's device, often used secretly to capture sensitive information.
  • Legitimate Uses: Sometimes used by parents, law enforcement, or IT technicians for monitoring or troubleshooting.
  • Malicious Uses: Attackers use keyloggers to steal passwords, banking details, capture screenshots, and monitor browsing activities, then send this data to a remote server.

Types of Keyloggers

  1. Software-Based Keyloggers
    • Functionality: Capture keystrokes and may also record on-screen keyboard input and track websites where login details are entered.
    • Distribution: Often spread through malware, phishing emails, or malicious websites and can be bundled with other spyware.
    • Legitimate Uses: Used by IT technicians for diagnostics and by researchers to study writing processes (e.g., programs like Inputlog and Scriptlog).
    • Prevalence: More common than hardware-based keyloggers due to ease of distribution.
  1. Hardware-Based Keyloggers
    • Functionality: Operate independently of software by being physically connected to the device (e.g., USB drive).
    • Usage: Can be discreetly plugged into a device and left until the attacker retrieves the stored data.
    • Prevalence: Less common now due to the availability of software-based keyloggers, but still in use by attackers.

Protection Against Keyloggers

  • System Security: Regularly update system and antivirus software to detect and block known keylogging threats.
  • Use of Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): MFA adds a layer of protection, requiring a second factor (e.g., phone or biometrics) that attackers cannot easily capture with a keylogger.
  • One-Time Passwords (OTP): Some websites offer OTP logins, which send a temporary password to a trusted device, reducing the risk of reuse by attackers.

Darknets Overview

  • Definition: Darknets (or the dark web) are networks of websites that require specialized software to access. They enable private and anonymous internet activity, which can be used legally or illegally.
  • Access: Not accessible with standard web browsers; require technology like Tor, which uses multiple proxies and encryption to conceal user identities.

Major Darknets

  1. Tor: The largest and best-known darknet, though limited to about 10,000 active sites. Tor obscures user and site identities, offering high anonymity.
  1. Other Darknets: Include networks like I2P, Freenet, GNUnet, and ZeroNet, which provide similar privacy protections but are smaller than Tor.

Content on Darknets

  • Illegal Goods and Services: Often include narcotics, weapons, stolen financial information, fake IDs, hacking services, and even assassinations.
  • Legal Content: Can range from forums for tech discussions, websites for human rights activists, to general, innocuous sites like those sharing pictures or supporting political dissent.

Legal Status of Darknets

  • Legality: Accessing darknets is generally legal, but transactions or services purchased there may be illegal, depending on local laws.
  • Caution: Users should be cautious due to potential exposure to disturbing or harmful content.

Benefits of Using Darknets

  1. Anonymity: Enables users to remain anonymous, often bypassing standard surveillance.
  1. Untraceability: Protects users from being easily located or identified.
  1. Illegal Opportunities: Provides a space for activities that would be restricted or illegal on the open web.

Threats on Darknets

Users face multiple security risks, including:

  • Keyloggers
  • Ransomware
  • Phishing and malware
  • Scams

Browsing darknets exposes users to risks from hackers and criminals who use the dark web’s anonymity for malicious activities.

Overview of USB Devices

  • Definition: USB (Universal Serial Bus) devices are commonly used to connect peripherals (e.g., keyboards, mice) and charge mobile devices.
  • Vulnerability: The widespread use of USB devices provides opportunities for malicious actors to exploit them, leading to serious security risks.

USB Attack Techniques

  1. Keystroke Injection Attacks
    • Method: Uses malware to input commands on a victim's device, potentially installing further malware or stealing data.
    • Implementation: Often executed through rogue USB devices connected physically to a target device.

Types of Malicious USB Devices

  1. Rubber Ducky
    • Description: A USB device that mimics a keyboard to perform keystroke injection attacks.
    • Capabilities: Can execute and dump malicious payloads, including keylogging to capture sensitive data like credentials.
  1. BadUSB
    • Description: A cheaper variant of Rubber Ducky that disguises itself as a harmless device (e.g., mouse or keyboard).
    • Function: Mimics human interface devices (HID) and gains trusted access to a target computer, making it more challenging to detect.
  1. Bash Bunny
    • Description: A USB device capable of multiple emulations, including keyboard and Ethernet adaptor.
    • Features: Has an LED indicator and a three-position switch to manage multiple payloads; can provide access to Ethernet networks (LAN).
  1. O.MG Cable
    • Description: A cable that resembles a standard charging cable but includes a remote-controlled chip for attacks.
    • Characteristics: Allows data and power transfer to appear normal, making it harder for victims to detect malicious actions.
  1. Killer USB
    • Purpose: Unlike other devices, it's designed solely to destroy devices by releasing a high voltage, causing damage to internal components.
    • Mechanism: Charges capacitors and discharges electricity back into the device, damaging sensitive parts.

Protection Against Malicious USB Devices

  • Avoid Unknown Devices: Never plug in unfamiliar USB devices you find.
  • Organizational Policies: Some organizations disable USB ports via software policies to mitigate USB-based attacks.
  • USB Blockers: Physical blockers can be installed in USB ports to prevent unauthorized access.

Overview of Virtual Card Numbers

  • Definition: Virtual credit cards are digital equivalents of physical credit cards, featuring a 16-digit card number and CVV but without a physical form.
  • Function: They provide an additional layer of security by using unique card numbers that can be specific to a single merchant or set to expire after a brief time.

Types of Virtual Card Numbers

  1. Merchant-Specific Virtual Cards
    • Usage: Can be used repeatedly with a specific merchant, linking back to the user's main bank account.
    • Benefit: Limits potential fraud to only that merchant if card details are compromised, making it easier to track the source of any breach.
  1. Timed Virtual Cards
    • Usage: Designed for single transactions on sites or with companies the user might not fully trust.
    • Expiry: Typically expire after 2-10 minutes, providing security against unauthorized reuse.

Benefits of Virtual Card Numbers

  • Enhanced Security: Helps prevent fraud by isolating transactions and limiting the scope of exposure.
  • Protection in E-commerce: Useful for online transactions, where the risk of fraud from data breaches is higher.
  • Easily Monitored: Each merchant has a unique virtual card, allowing for quick detection of compromised information.

Limitations

  • Not Effective Against:
    • Phishing Attacks: Virtual card numbers don't prevent phishing scams.
    • Malware: Cannot stop malware attacks that might steal information from a device.
    • In-Store Transactions: Virtual cards aren’t usable in physical stores, making physical credit cards still necessary.

Examples of Providers for Virtual Card Services

  1. Privacy.com: Provides virtual cards linked to bank accounts or debit cards (U.S. only).
  1. Revolut: Offers disposable virtual cards that generate new card details after each transaction.
  1. Apple Pay and Google Pay: Use randomly generated card numbers for contactless payment, though card details don’t change with each use.
  1. PayPal: Offers a virtual credit card with additional security features, but without disposable numbers.
