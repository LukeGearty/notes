Data Privacy

What is Personal Data?

  • Personal information (PI) or personal data refers to data that can identify an individual, such as social media pictures or an email address.
  • Personal data includes information that may not seem identifying by itself but can become identifying when combined with other data (e.g., first name + address + phone number).
  • Sensitive Personally Identifiable Information (Sensitive PII) is a subset of PI that requires stricter regulations for collection and usage (e.g., ethnic origin, criminal offenses, biometric data).
  • Sensitive PII is defined differently across regions, with examples like the European GDPR (which includes special category data) and the California CCPA (which doesn't explicitly define sensitive PII).
  • Data protection ensures data is secured, and data privacy governs how data is collected, used, and shared.
  • Data privacy legislation aims to ensure data is used for the intended purpose and protected from misuse.

What is Privacy?

  • Personal data belongs to the individual, even when it's in the possession of an organization. Organizations must not use data (e.g., for marketing) without permission.
  • Lack of control over data usage can lead to vulnerabilities, such as fraud or unauthorized sharing.
  • Data privacy laws safeguard privacy by ensuring data is handled properly and not shared without consent.
  • Organizations must prioritize data privacy to maintain trust and avoid legal issues or fines.

What is Consent?

  • Valid consent (meaningful consent) must be obtained for data usage, typically through a privacy notice that explains the data being collected, its purpose, and sharing details.
  • Consent gives individuals control over how their data is used, and organizations are held accountable for any violations.

Subject Data Rights:

  • Data subjects have rights under data privacy laws to control how their data is handled, including:
    • Right to be Informed: Organizations must inform individuals about data collection and sharing.
    • Right to Access: Individuals can request a copy of the data an organization holds about them.
    • Right to Deletion: Individuals can request their data be deleted under certain conditions (e.g., if it's no longer needed or consent is withdrawn).
  • These rights vary by legislation, so it's important to consult internal documentation or relevant laws to ensure compliance.

Why Does Data Privacy Matter?

1. It's a Human Right

  • Privacy is essential for maintaining freedom of thought, speech, and actions.
  • Without privacy, everything you say or do could be under scrutiny.
  • Edward Snowden (2015): "When you say ‘I don’t care about the right to privacy because I have nothing to hide’, that’s no different than saying ‘I don’t care about freedom of speech because I have nothing to say’."
  • Data privacy specifically refers to the protection of digital personal information, ensuring its proper collection and use.

2. Data is Valuable

  • Meglena Kuvena (2009): "Personal data is the new oil of the internet and the new currency of the digital world."
  • Personal data is one of the most valuable commodities today, and organizations will go to great lengths to acquire it.
  • Personal data includes elements like name, birthdate, medical history, and more, creating a digital identity.
  • Digital identity: A collection of online data about an individual, including personal data and digital behaviors like browsing history.
  • Businesses use digital identities to create targeted advertising campaigns that are more likely to engage individuals based on their interests, generating more revenue.

3. What Happens if Personal Data Isn't Properly Protected?

  • Poor data protection can result in identity theft, discrimination, and even physical harm.
    • Identity Theft: Fraudulent activities like taking out credit cards in someone's name.
    • Discrimination: Potential job loss or discrimination based on data such as age, race, or gender.
    • Physical Harm: Exposure of personal or geolocation data can lead to physical attacks.
  • The Facebook–Cambridge Analytica Scandal:
    • Cambridge Analytica used data from nearly 300,000 Facebook users to create targeted political ads for the 2016 US presidential election.
    • Data was obtained through an app called “Thisisyourdigitallife,” which secretly collected data from users’ Facebook friends.
    • Although users consented for research purposes, the data was sold to Cambridge Analytica for marketing.

4. How Can I Protect Personal Data?

  • At Work:
    • Follow company policies on data collection, usage, privacy, and protection.
    • Lock your computer when stepping away from your desk.
    • Ensure sensitive documents are securely stored at the end of the day.
  • At Home:
    • Use a VPN for secure internet browsing.
    • Avoid reusing passwords; use a password manager.
    • Enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) when possible.
    • Be cautious with sharing online and set social media profiles to private.
    • Stay aware of phishing methods and scams.
