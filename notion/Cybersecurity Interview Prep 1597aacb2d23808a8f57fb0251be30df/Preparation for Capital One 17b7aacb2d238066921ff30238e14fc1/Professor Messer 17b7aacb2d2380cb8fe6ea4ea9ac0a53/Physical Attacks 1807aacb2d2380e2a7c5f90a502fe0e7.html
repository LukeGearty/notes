Physical Attacks

Old-school security

Physical attacks involve direct manipulation, damage, or unauthorized access to hardware, facilities, or devices. These attacks bypass digital security measures and exploit the physical environment of the target.

Types of Physical Attacks:

1. Theft and Tampering

  • Description: Unauthorized individuals steal or tamper with devices to access data or install malicious components.
  • Examples:
    • Stealing laptops, smartphones, or storage devices containing sensitive data.
    • Installing hardware keyloggers or USB drives with malicious payloads.
  • Prevention:
    • Use physical locks and cable locks for devices.
    • Implement access controls for secure areas.
    • Encrypt sensitive data to prevent access even if devices are stolen.

2. Shoulder Surfing

  • Description: Observing a userâ€™s screen or keyboard to capture sensitive information, such as passwords or PINs.
  • Examples:
    • Watching someone enter a password at an ATM or login screen.
  • Prevention:
    • Use privacy screens for monitors.
    • Be aware of your surroundings in public spaces.

3. Dumpster Diving

  • Description: Searching through discarded items (e.g., papers, hardware, or storage media) to extract confidential information.
  • Examples:
    • Finding printed documents with personal information or passwords.
    • Recovering data from improperly discarded storage devices.
  • Prevention:
    • Shred sensitive documents before disposal.
    • Properly wipe and destroy storage devices before discarding.

4. Eavesdropping

  • Description: Listening to conversations or electronic signals to gain unauthorized information.
  • Examples:
    • Using devices to intercept phone calls or network traffic.
    • Listening to office conversations to extract sensitive details.
  • Prevention:
    • Use soundproof rooms for sensitive discussions.
    • Encrypt communications to prevent interception.

5. Tailgating (Piggybacking)

  • Description: Gaining unauthorized entry into a secure area by following an authorized person.
  • Examples:
    • Walking behind someone who swipes a keycard for access.
  • Prevention:
    • Train employees to prevent tailgating and challenge unknown individuals.
    • Use turnstiles or mantraps to restrict entry.

6. Hardware Implants

  • Description: Installing unauthorized hardware components to spy on or manipulate systems.
  • Examples:
    • Rogue USB devices injecting malicious code.
    • Hardware keyloggers capturing keystrokes.
  • Prevention:
    • Physically inspect devices regularly for tampering.
    • Disable unused ports or use USB port locks.

7. Sabotage

  • Description: Physically damaging devices, infrastructure, or networks to disrupt operations.
  • Examples:
    • Cutting network cables or power lines.
    • Damaging servers or hard drives.
  • Prevention:
    • Secure critical infrastructure in locked cabinets or rooms.
    • Use surveillance systems to monitor facilities.

8. Social Engineering

  • Description: Manipulating individuals into granting physical access or revealing information.
  • Examples:
    • Impersonating IT personnel to gain access to restricted areas.
    • Posing as delivery personnel to leave infected hardware.
  • Prevention:
    • Train employees to verify identities and request proper credentials.
    • Establish strict visitor policies.

General Prevention Strategies:

  • Access Controls:
    • Use keycards, biometric scanners, or PIN pads to secure areas.
  • Surveillance:
    • Install CCTV cameras and alarm systems to deter attackers.
  • Awareness Training:
    • Educate employees about physical security risks and prevention techniques.
  • Device Hardening:
    • Lock devices and use encryption to protect data.
  • Incident Response:
    • Develop protocols for responding to physical security breaches.
