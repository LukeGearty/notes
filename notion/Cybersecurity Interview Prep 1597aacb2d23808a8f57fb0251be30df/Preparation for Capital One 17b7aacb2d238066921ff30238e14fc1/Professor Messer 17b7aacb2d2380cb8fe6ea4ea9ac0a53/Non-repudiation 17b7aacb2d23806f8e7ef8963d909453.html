A digital signature is a cryptographic technique used to ensure the authenticity, integrity, and non-repudiation of digital messages or documents. It is the digital equivalent of a handwritten signature or a wax seal but offers much stronger security.

Key Concepts

  1. Authenticity: Verifies the sender's identity.
  1. Integrity: Ensures the message has not been tampered with.
  1. Non-repudiation: Prevents the sender from denying they sent the message.

How It Works

Digital signatures rely on public-key cryptography (asymmetric encryption), which involves:

  • A private key (kept secret by the signer).
  • A public key (shared with others to verify the signature).

Example

Imagine Alice wants to send Bob an important document securely.

  1. Generating the Digital Signature:
    • Alice uses a hash function (e.g., SHA-256) to create a fixed-size hash (a digital fingerprint) of the document.
    • She encrypts this hash with her private key, creating the digital signature.
  1. Sending the Document:
    • Alice sends Bob the document along with the digital signature.
  1. Verifying the Signature:
    • Bob uses Alice’s public key to decrypt the signature, retrieving the hash.
    • Bob hashes the received document using the same hash function Alice used.
    • He compares the decrypted hash with his computed hash:
      • If they match, the document is authentic and unaltered.
      • If they don’t match, the document may have been tampered with or the signature is invalid.

Practical Use Case

Digital signatures are commonly used in:

  • Email communication (e.g., signing emails to verify the sender).
  • Software distribution (verifying the integrity of downloaded software).
  • Legal documents (ensuring the authenticity of electronic contracts).
