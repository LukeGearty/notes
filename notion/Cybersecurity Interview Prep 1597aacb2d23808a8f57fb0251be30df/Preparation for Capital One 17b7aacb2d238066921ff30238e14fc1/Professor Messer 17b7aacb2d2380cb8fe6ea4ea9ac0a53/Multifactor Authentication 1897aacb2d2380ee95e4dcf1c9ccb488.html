Authentication Factors Notes

Definition:

Authentication factors are different parameters used to verify a user’s identity when logging into a system. Common authentication factors include:

  • Something you know
  • Something you have
  • Something you are
  • Somewhere you are

1. Something You Know

  • Relies on knowledge unique to the user.
  • Examples:
    • Passwords: Memorized strings or phrases known only to the user.
    • Personal Identification Numbers (PINs): E.g., a 4-digit PIN entered when using an ATM.
    • Unlock Patterns: Used on mobile devices or tablets.
  • Characteristics:
    • Only the user knows this information.
    • Often used as the most common authentication factor.

2. Something You Have

  • Relies on physical possession of an object or device.
  • Examples:
    • Smart Cards: IDs integrated with smart card technology; often paired with a PIN for multi-factor authentication.
    • USB Security Keys: Contain a unique certificate tied to the user.
    • Hardware Tokens: Generate random codes synchronized with the server.
    • Software Tokens: Mobile phone apps that generate codes, removing the need for extra devices.
    • Mobile Phones: Often used for SMS or text-based codes during login.
  • Characteristics:
    • Physical or digital possession required.
    • Often combined with other factors for security.

3. Something You Are

  • Relies on biometric information unique to the user.
  • Examples:
    • Fingerprints
    • Voiceprints
    • Other Biometric Data
  • How It Works:
    • A mathematical representation of the biometric is stored, not the actual image or recording.
  • Challenges:
    • Difficult to modify or change (e.g., fingerprints, voiceprints).
    • Vulnerable to circumvention; typically combined with other factors for enhanced security.

4. Somewhere You Are

  • Relies on geolocation to verify a user’s location.
  • Examples:
    • GPS Location: Mobile devices determine physical location.
    • IP Address: Can approximate location but may not be precise.
    • Geolocation Services: Combine GPS and IP data for a more accurate result.
  • Use Cases:
    • Prevents logins from suspicious locations (e.g., attempting to log in from a country far from the user’s current location).
  • Challenges:
    • IPv6 makes tracking more complex.

Key Takeaways

  • Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): Often combines two or more factors (e.g., a password + smart card + fingerprint).
  • Authentication Factor Strength:
    • Biometric factors (“Something you are”) are harder to replicate but not foolproof.
    • Combining factors increases security and reduces vulnerability to breaches.
  • Practical Examples:
    • ATM access: PIN (“something you know”) + debit card (“something you have”).
    • Secure login: Password (“something you know”) + SMS code (“something you have”) + location check (“somewhere you are”).
