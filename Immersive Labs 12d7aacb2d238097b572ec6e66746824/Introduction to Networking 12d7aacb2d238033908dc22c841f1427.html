Introduction to Networking

What is a Network?

Network Overview

  • Definition: A network is a connection of two or more electronic devices (e.g., computers, mobile phones, smart TVs, gaming consoles) using cables or Wi-Fi. The Internet is a large example, connecting millions of devices worldwide.

History of Networks

  • 1970s: Robert Kahn and Vinton Cerf developed the Transmission Control Protocol and Internet Protocol (TCP/IP), a foundational communication model for data transmission across networks.
  • 1983: ARPANET adopted TCP/IP, paving the way for the modern Internet, often called the "network of networks."

Common Network Types

  1. Local Area Network (LAN):
    • Limited to small areas like schools or offices.
  1. Wide Area Network (WAN):
    • Spans larger areas, connecting networks across cities or countries through infrastructure like transoceanic cables or satellites.

Role of Servers in Networks

  • Servers: Dedicated devices that provide resources and services to other network devices (e.g., file storage, security, messaging). They are typically accessed indirectly by users.

Advantages of Networks

  1. Cost-Effective: Multiple devices can share a single network, reducing costs.
  1. Communication: Networks allow communication through email and instant messaging (e.g., Slack, Teams), improving efficiency.
  1. Data Sharing: Information and files can be easily shared across networked devices.
  1. Online Transactions: Networks support secure online banking and transactions, making everyday tasks more convenient.

Disadvantages of Networks

  1. Virus and Malware Risks: Networks facilitate easy sharing, but they also increase the risk of spreading viruses and malware.
  1. Vulnerabilities: Remote access introduces potential security risks, especially on Internet-connected networks.
  1. Complexity: Setting up and maintaining networks can be challenging, requiring skilled personnel for effective management.

Types of Networks

Types of Networks

  1. Local Area Network (LAN):
    • Limited to a small area like a home or office.
    • Often used to share resources such as printers or internet connections within that space.
    • Example: Home or office network.
  1. Wireless Local Area Network (WLAN):
    • A wireless version of LAN, commonly known as Wi-Fi.
    • Transfers data wirelessly at high speeds using routers or modems.
    • Example: Wi-Fi networks at home or in public spaces like cafes.
  1. Wide Area Network (WAN):
    • Connects networks over larger geographic areas, such as cities or countries.
    • Useful for companies with offices in multiple locations, allowing unified network access.
    • Example: The Internet, the largest WAN.

Wired vs. Wireless Networks

  1. Wired Networks:
    • Data travels through physical cables (e.g., Ethernet) to connect devices.
    • Provides stable, high-speed connections but may require extensive cabling.
  1. Wireless Networks:
    • Uses radio waves (e.g., Wi-Fi) for data transmission.
    • Convenient and easy to expand without additional wiring, ideal for public areas or homes.

Other Network Types

  1. Personal Area Network (PAN):
    • Connects devices within a user’s immediate vicinity (range of a few centimeters to meters).
    • Example: Bluetooth connection between a phone and a wireless headset.
  1. Virtual Private Network (VPN):
    • Encrypts network traffic and hides a user's IP address to secure data on public networks.
    • Example: VPN software for secure internet browsing on public Wi-Fi.
  1. Body Area Network (BAN):
    • Used in medical applications to track or support biological functions with sensors.
    • Example: A wireless insulin pump for diabetic patients.
  1. Global National Satellite System (GNSS):
    • Uses satellites for GPS tracking, providing data on location, speed, time, and direction.
    • Example: GPS in smartphones and pet trackers.

What is Network Hardware?

  • Definition: Network hardware includes all devices like computers, peripherals, interface cards, and equipment needed for data-processing and communications in a network.
  • Components: Common networking hardware includes routers, hubs, switches, bridges, modems, and other interface devices.

Key Network Devices and Functions

  1. Router
    • Function: Connects different networks, allowing communication between them.
    • Capabilities: Often includes hubs, switches, modems, and wireless access points.
    • Types:
      • Wired Routers: Connect to the network using ethernet cables.
      • Wireless Routers: Broadcast Wi-Fi signals, enabling cable-free connectivity for devices.
    • Purpose: Routes data packets to the correct destination. Acts as the network's default gateway, essential for communication with other networks.
  1. Modem
    • Function: Connects a network to the internet.
    • Difference from Router: A modem provides the connection to the internet, while a router distributes this connection to multiple devices within the network. Often combined in one device but are separate technologies.
  1. Hub
    • Function: Acts as a central connection point for devices like computers and printers.
    • Operation: Passes all network traffic to every connected device without routing or filtering.
    • Limitations: Lack of intelligence to direct traffic leads to excess network traffic.
  1. Switch
    • Function: Connects devices within a Local Area Network (LAN).
    • Capabilities:
      • Provides centralized ports for connecting multiple devices.
      • Uses MAC addresses to direct traffic intelligently, minimizing network congestion.
    • Types:
      • Managed Switches: Allow configuration options, such as disabling ports and adjusting speeds.
      • Unmanaged Switches: Basic connection with no configurable options.
  1. Other Hardware
    • Dongle: A portable USB device that connects to mobile networks for broadband access on the go. Often replaced by smartphone hotspotting or wireless hotspots due to limited wireless capability.

Domain Name System (DNS)

  • Definition: DNS is a distributed, hierarchical directory that matches domain names to IP addresses.
  • Analogy: Like a phonebook, DNS links human-readable names (e.g., immersivelabs.online) to IP addresses, making it easier to access websites without memorizing numerical IP addresses.

DNS Servers and Their Roles

Four types of DNS servers work together to provide an IP address for a domain:

  1. Recursive DNS Server
    • Often managed by the ISP or third parties, it initiates the DNS query and checks its cache for stored IP address information.
  1. Root Name Server
    • The first stop after the recursive server if the IP address isn’t cached. It provides a list of authoritative name servers for the requested top-level domain (TLD).
  1. Top-Level Domain (TLD) Name Server
    • Directs the query to the specific authoritative name server for the domain.
  1. Authoritative Name Server
    • Stores the actual DNS records and returns the specific IP address for the requested domain.

How DNS Works

  1. Initiating Query: User types a URL into a browser. If visited previously, the local DNS cache returns the response.
  1. Recursive Resolver: If no cache exists, the recursive DNS server checks for the record, moving on to the root name server if necessary.
  1. Root Name Server: Returns the TLD server list for the query.
  1. TLD and Authoritative Servers: Narrow the search to the authoritative server, which provides the final IP address.
  1. Result: The IP address is returned to the user, displaying the requested webpage.

DNS Records and Their Types

DNS records, or zone files, instruct the DNS server on how to handle domain requests. Each record has a Time to Live (TTL) value, which specifies when the recursive server must refresh the record.

  1. A Record (Address Record)
    • Provides the IP address associated with a domain name.
  1. CNAME Record (Canonical Name Record)
    • Redirects a subdomain (e.g., yellow.immersivelabs.online) to the root domain, simplifying domain management.
  1. MX Record (Mail Exchanger Record)
    • Directs email to designated servers and manages email handling for a domain.
  1. TXT Record
    • Allows storing text information. Originally used for human-readable notes, it can also hold machine-readable data for various purposes.

Importance of DNS

  • Usability: Simplifies internet navigation by allowing users to remember readable domain names instead of complex IP addresses.
  • Efficiency: DNS enables one domain to have multiple IP addresses, optimizing website accessibility and redundancy.

What are Network Topologies?

  • Definition: Network topology describes the layout and organization of devices and their connections in a network.
  • Purpose: Defines how devices connect and communicate over a network, impacting network performance, scalability, and fault tolerance.

Types of Network Topologies

  1. Bus Topology
    • Structure: Devices are connected along a single central cable (backbone).
    • Data Flow: Data travels in one direction, with terminators at each end to prevent signal bounce.
    • Use Case: Simple setup, but less common today, sometimes used to connect peripheral devices like printers.
    • Limitations: Not very scalable, and any break in the backbone disrupts the entire network.
  1. Star Topology
    • Structure: All devices connect to a central hub or switch.
    • Data Flow: Communication goes through the hub, which manages data traffic between devices.
    • Advantages: Enhanced security, as data only passes through the hub. Isolates individual failures to one device.
    • Drawbacks: If the hub fails, the entire network goes down (single point of failure).
    • Common Example: Local Area Network (LAN).
  1. Ring Topology
    • Structure: Devices form a closed-loop (ring), connecting each device to its neighbors.
    • Data Flow: Data moves in one or both directions around the ring.
    • Drawbacks: Vulnerable to failure if one device goes down, as it disrupts the network. Not easily scalable.
    • Maintenance: Adding or removing devices requires taking the network offline.
  1. Tree Topology
    • Structure: Combines elements of bus and star topologies in a hierarchical, tree-like arrangement.
    • Data Flow: Data flows from one level of the hierarchy to another, enabling flexible network expansion.
    • Advantages: Scalable and flexible; suitable for large networks like WANs.
    • Drawbacks: Dependent on root nodes; if the main nodes fail, sections of the network could be affected.
  1. Mesh Topology
    • Structure: Devices are interconnected, creating either a full mesh (all devices connected) or partial mesh (some interconnected).
    • Advantages: High reliability, as multiple pathways prevent network failure if one connection drops.
    • Drawbacks: Complex to set up, with high cabling requirements, making it time-consuming and costly.
    • Common Use: Often used for high-performance or highly secure networks.
  1. Hybrid Topology
    • Structure: Combines two or more topologies, offering flexibility to suit specific organizational needs.
    • Examples:
      • Star-Ring Hybrid: Combines star and ring topologies, allowing multiple star networks to connect via a central ring.
      • Office Scenario: Departments using different topologies (e.g., IT uses star, finance uses ring) create a hybrid when interconnected.
    • Advantages: Customizable and adaptable for large, complex network environments.
