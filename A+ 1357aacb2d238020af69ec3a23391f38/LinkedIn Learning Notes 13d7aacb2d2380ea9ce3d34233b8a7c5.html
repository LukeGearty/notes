Machine Language

  • Codebook:
    • A predefined set of binary patterns represents specific instructions (e.g., move a number to a register, perform addition).
    • This is the CPU's machine language.
  • Registers:
    • Internal storage for temporary data during calculations.
    • Examples:
      • AX, BX, CX, DX (general-purpose registers).

Executing Instructions

  1. Load Values into Registers:
    • Example: Load 2 into AX, 3 into BX.
    • Use the clock signal to process each step.
  1. Perform Operations:
    • Use a codebook command to add values in AX and BX.
    • Result stored in AX.
  1. Output Results:
    • Use another command to display AX's value on the external data bus.

Clock Signal

  • Acts as a "doorbell" to synchronize the CPU's actions.
  • Each clock cycle processes a step.
  • Complex commands may require multiple cycles to complete.

Pipeline Processing

  • CPUs handle tasks in parallel using a pipeline:
    • Commands are divided into discrete steps.
    • Multiple tasks are processed simultaneously by specialized units:
      • Integer math.
      • Floating-point operations.
      • Data movement (e.g., to/from registers or RAM).
  • Modern Pipelines:
    • Can have 16–30+ steps.
    • Efficiency depends on how well tasks are divided and processed.

Understanding x86 and x64

  1. Definition
    • x86 (32-bit): Refers to a 32-bit Instruction Set Architecture (ISA). Processes data in 32-bit chunks.
    • x64 (64-bit): Refers to a 64-bit ISA. Processes data in 64-bit chunks.
  1. Key Differences
    • Data Handling Capacity:
      • 32-bit: Can handle 232 (approximately 4 billion) bits of data at once.

        2322^{32}

      • 64-bit: Can handle 264 bits of data, or 4 billion times the capacity of 32-bit systems.

        2642^{64}

    • Bus Width:
      • 32-bit: Narrow "lane" for data transfer.
      • 64-bit: Wider "lane," doubling the data transfer rate.
  1. Performance Advantages of 64-bit
    • Security: Enhanced security features.
    • Graphics: Improved graphics performance.
    • Overall Output: Superior system performance and data processing.

Categories of Instruction Sets

  1. x86 (32-bit only): Supports only 32-bit operations.
  1. x86-x64 (Hybrid): Primarily supports 64-bit but is backward compatible with 32-bit.
  1. x64 (64-bit only): Supports only 64-bit operations.
  1. IA-32:

  • Why CPU Cooling Matters: The CPU generates significant heat, and if cooling systems like heat sinks and fans are clogged, they can't transfer heat effectively. This leads to overheating, causing potential system failure.
  • Heat Sink Overview:
    • Heat sinks have a wide base and multiple pipes leading to fins.
    • The goal is to make flush contact with the CPU and use thermal paste for efficient thermal transfer to the fins.
    • Heat sinks vary in design, including multi-fin and multi-pipe types. They can be made from materials like copper and are sometimes customized for specific needs.
  • Fans in Cooling:
    • Most cooling fans are 120mm, with some larger models at 180mm.
    • 120mm fans are the standard for general-purpose cooling, while larger ones are used for case cooling.
  • Thermal Paste:
    • There are five main types: silicone-based, ceramic-based, carbon-based, metal-based, and liquid metal-based (which is more advanced and requires caution).
    • Thermal paste fills in the microscopic imperfections in the CPU lid and heat sink base to enable efficient heat transfer.
    • Application: The speaker recommends the "Intel method" for applying thermal paste, which involves using a small amount (pea-sized drop). The goal is to avoid over-application and ensure even coverage.
    • A common mistake is using too much paste, which can spill over and create uneven coverage. Practice applying thermal paste with a stress putty or index card.
  • Cleaning and Reapplying Thermal Paste:
    • If too much paste is used, clean the CPU using isopropyl alcohol (70%) and a Q-tip, wiping away excess paste carefully.
    • Thermal cleaning kits or alcohol can be used for this process.
  • Thermal Pads:
    • Often used to replace pre-installed thermal pads in GPUs, thermal pads can be made of materials like copper, silicone, or wax and are less efficient than paste.
  • Cooling Types:
    • Air Cooling: The speaker's preferred method, using a Noctua DH14 cooler. It's effective, though a fan failure won't harm the system.
    • Liquid Cooling: More efficient, with a pump that circulates liquid (typically water mixed with alcohol) through a radiator to cool the CPU. Liquid cooling provides better thermal transfer and quieter operation, though it's more complex and requires careful maintenance.
  • Summary: Key ways to keep a PC cool are maintaining clean fans and heat sinks, properly applying thermal paste, and considering liquid cooling for more efficient heat dissipation.

Notes: RAM Capacity and Configuration

General Concepts

  • RAM stores a specific amount of data (measured in bytes).
  • Capacity is independent of RAM type (DDR, DDR2, DDR3, etc.), which indicates speed.
  • RAM chips are organized internally as a square, and capacity increases quadratically:
    • Doubling each side quadruples capacity.
    • Example progression: 256MB → 1GB → 4GB → 16GB.

Single-Sided vs. Double-Sided RAM

  • Single-sided RAM: Chips are only on one side of the stick.
  • Double-sided RAM: Chips on both sides of the stick.
    • Offers higher capacity in the same form factor.
    • Some motherboards may not support double-sided RAM. Always check the motherboard's compatibility guide.

Motherboard Slots and Channels

  • Motherboards have a limited number of slots (e.g., 2, 4, 8, or 16 on servers).
  • Total system RAM = Sum of capacities of all installed sticks.
    • Example: 2 sticks of 8GB = 16GB total; 4 sticks of 8GB = 32GB total.

Memory Channels

  • Dual-channel memory (common in modern motherboards):
    • Requires pairs of RAM sticks with identical capacity and speed in specific slots.
    • Enhances performance by enabling simultaneous access to two sticks as if they were one.
  • Triple-channel: Rare, requires three sticks in specific slots.
  • Single-channel mode: Some motherboards allow it but significantly reduce performance.

Best Practices for Channel Configuration

  • Install identical RAM sticks (same size and speed) in the same channel.
  • Mismatched RAM across channels can work but may reduce efficiency.
  • Always consult the motherboard manual to determine the correct slot configuration:
    • Example: Slots closest to the CPU are usually Channel A.

Upgrading RAM

  • If slots are full, you must replace smaller-capacity sticks with larger ones.
  • Example:
    • A motherboard with 2 slots containing 4GB sticks has 8GB total.
    • To upgrade, replace the two 4GB sticks with larger-capacity sticks, e.g., two 8GB sticks for 16GB total.

Troubleshooting and Compatibility

  • If RAM is not configured correctly:
    • The system may fail to boot or display a "single-channel mode" error.
  • Avoid mixing RAM speeds and capacities across channels when possible.
  • Always verify the motherboard's compatibility with RAM types, sizes, and configurations.

Key Takeaways

  • RAM capacity and performance are determined by the motherboard's slot count, channel configuration, and compatibility.
  • Proper configuration maximizes system performance and efficiency.

Motherboard Overview

Basic Input/Output System (BIOS)

  • Purpose: BIOS allows the computer to communicate with hardware before the operating system (OS) loads.
    • It provides built-in language to interact with hardware even without OS drivers.
    • BIOS is stored in firmware (programming stored on a chip), unlike software, which is stored on other media.
  • History and Evolution:
    • BIOS is a part of the motherboard, and all motherboards include it.
    • Early BIOSes were on a single, large chip.
      • Example: A BIOS chip from a 1990s motherboard.
  • Modern BIOS:
    • Modern systems often include two BIOS chips, providing redundancy.
      • If one BIOS is corrupted, the backup BIOS can be used.
    • M-BIOS and B-BIOS are labels for the two copies (used in modern motherboards like those from Gigabyte).

Key Functions of BIOS

  1. Mass Storage Device Communication:
    • BIOS allows the computer to communicate with hard drives, SSDs, etc., before the OS loads.
    • Useful for testing storage devices without booting into the OS.
  1. Power-On Self-Test (POST):
    • BIOS performs POST during boot-up to check the health and functionality of critical hardware components.
    • Ensures devices like hard drives and monitors are functioning before loading the OS.
  1. System Setup and CMOS:
    • BIOS includes code for configuring hardware settings, such as hard drive capacity and configuration.
    • The system setup is also known as CMOS (Complementary Metal-Oxide Semiconductor), which stores these settings.

BIOS Chip Characteristics

  • BIOS is a small chip on the motherboard that interacts with the hardware.
  • Early BIOS was large and visible on older motherboards.
  • Modern systems feature a more compact design with backup BIOS chips for reliability.

Overview

  • The motherboard is a crucial PC component, though its installation can involve some troubleshooting.
  • The CompTIA A+ troubleshooting theory process can help resolve installation issues systematically.

Preparation

Electrostatic Discharge (ESD) Protection

  • ESD precautions:
    • Use an ESD mat or an ESD strap.
    • The ESD strap should be in contact with your skin and clipped to a metal surface to ground static buildup.

Installation Steps

Standoffs

  • Purpose of standoffs:
    • Prevent the motherboard from directly contacting the metal case, avoiding short circuits.
    • Provide stable mounting for the motherboard.
  • Ensure all required standoffs are installed in the case.

I/O Shield

  • Purpose:
    • Acts as a dust blocker and aligns with the motherboard's I/O ports.
  • Installation tips:
    • Ensure the correct orientation.
    • Be careful when handling as edges can be sharp.

Installing the Motherboard

  1. Align the motherboard with the standoffs and the I/O shield.
  1. Use screws to secure the motherboard:
    • Tighten screws incrementally across all points rather than fully securing one at a time.

Troubleshooting Installation Issues

  • Example Issue:
    • Misalignment of the motherboard and I/O shield.
  • Troubleshooting Steps (CompTIA A+):
    1. Identify the problem:
      • Misalignment between the motherboard and I/O shield.
    1. Test the theory:
      • Check if the I/O shield is incorrectly positioned.
    1. Establish a plan of action:
      • Adjust the I/O shield orientation.
    1. Implement the solution:
      • Reinstall the motherboard with the corrected shield.
    1. Verify full functionality:
      • Ensure the motherboard is properly secured and functional.
    1. Document findings:
      • Record the issue and solution for future reference.

Defective Hardware Indicators

  1. Swelling Capacitors:
    • Capacitors hold a charge and are critical for motherboard functionality.
    • Signs of failure:
      • Swelling or bulging.
      • Accompanied by burning smells.
    • Action:
      • Immediately remove power and avoid further use of the defective motherboard.
  1. Burning Smells:
    • Could indicate a capacitor or other hardware failure.
    • Proper power handling is essential.

Additional Tips

  • Use an open-air case for demonstrations or troubleshooting.
  • For secure installations:
    • Adjust the motherboard slightly to ensure all holes align with standoffs before securing.
  • Tighten screws in a distributed manner, similar to securing lug nuts on a car tire.

Summary

  • Installing a motherboard involves ensuring proper ESD protection, using standoffs for safety, correctly aligning the I/O shield, and securing the board systematically.
  • Troubleshooting techniques like identifying issues, testing theories, and documenting solutions are essential.
  • Be vigilant for defective hardware signs, such as capacitor swelling or burning smells.

ATX Power Supply Overview

  • Function: The ATX power supply is the primary power source for modern desktop PCs, converting AC power into DC power (step-down transformer).
  • Voltage Input:
    • Plugged into a standard wall outlet (preferably through a surge suppressor).
    • On/Off Switch: Powers the power supply itself, not the computer.
    • Voltage Selector: A switch for 230V (Europe) and 120V (North America), but many power supplies now auto-sense the input voltage.
    • Fans: Power supplies typically control airflow in the system, with air coming from other parts of the case and exhausting through the power supply (though some modern designs may differ).

Power Supply Connectors

  • Primary ATX Connector: Powers the motherboard, with the original 20-pin and later 24-pin design for increased power needs.
    • 12V, 5V, and 3.3V: Color-coded connectors indicate power type:
      • Yellow = 12V
      • Red = 5V
      • Orange = 3.3V
  • ATX12V Extension: Adds additional power for more demanding CPUs. Some systems use one or two of these connectors depending on motherboard requirements.
  • Older Connectors:
    • Molex: General-purpose power connector, providing both 5V and 12V; used for various components, but increasingly obsolete.
    • Mini Connector: Originally for floppy drives, still used occasionally for specific components.
    • SATA Power Connector: Used for powering SATA drives (hard drives, optical drives).
    • PCIe Connector: Provides extra power to high-performance video cards.

Modular Power Supply Design

  • Modular Connectors: Power supplies often feature modular designs with detachable cables, allowing users to only use the necessary cables for their system, reducing cable clutter.
  • Cable Management: Modular cables are connected to the power supply as needed, simplifying installation and reducing unnecessary cables in the case.

Installation

  • Connector Chamfering: Cables are designed with chamfers (notches) to prevent incorrect connection (e.g., reversed cables).
  • Powering the System: Before connecting other components, ensure the necessary motherboard connectors are attached and use the modular cables based on system requirements.

    • Modular power supplies allow you to plug in and choose cables, which adds convenience. However, some purists argue that this can slightly reduce efficiency compared to soldered connections.
    • Despite the slight potential efficiency loss, many users prefer modular power supplies for their flexibility.
  1. ATX Standard:
    • The standard ATX power supply fits most systems, even many small form-factor systems.
    • Smaller ATX power supplies are available for compact systems, but they are not commonly tested in exams.

Cooling your system

The purpose of fans is to intake cool air and exhaust hot air

Fan Settings in BIOS

  1. Cable Management:
    • Proper cable management is essential for good airflow within the system. Cables should be routed behind the motherboard and only emerge where necessary to avoid obstructing airflow, which helps in keeping the system cool.
  1. Fan Placement and Airflow:
    • The cooling system relies on having intake and exhaust fans positioned correctly.
    • Intake Fans: Positioned at the front and possibly the bottom of the PC, these fans bring in cool air.
    • Exhaust Fans: Positioned at the back and top of the case, these fans expel hot or warm air from the system.
  1. Cleaning and Dust Removal:
    • Dust buildup can obstruct airflow and affect cooling. Use canned air or PC blowers (which don’t generate electrostatic discharge) to blow out dust from the system, ensuring smooth airflow.
  1. BIOS Thermal Settings:
    • Modern systems often have thermal settings in the BIOS that allow you to manage fan speeds and other cooling options.
    • In the BIOS, you can adjust fan speeds or enable Smart Fan Mode, which automatically adjusts fan speeds based on temperature, or manually control fan settings.
  1. Regular Maintenance:
    • Regularly open the case and clean the components to remove dust and debris, ensuring consistent airflow and effective cooling.

    • Confirm proper PSU functionality.
  1. Document Findings:
    • Record details about the issue and resolution for future reference.

    • Other components test as functional.

Mass Storage Technologies

Introduction to Mass Storage

1. Introduction to Storage Devices

  • Storage, also called mass storage, is used to store data like videos, games, and operating systems.
  • Types of storage devices:
    • Optical Media (e.g., CDs, DVDs)
    • Hard Disk Drives (HDDs) - spinning disks
    • Solid-State Drives (SSDs) - no moving parts, more popular now.
  • All storage devices store data in tiny units (minimum storage space) called:
    • Sectors (HDDs): 512 bytes or 4,096 bytes.
    • Blocks (SSDs): ~1,000 bytes.
    • Tracks (Optical Media): ~1,000 bytes.

2. Logical Block Addressing (LBA)

  • LBA: A controller (hardware + software) that interfaces between the operating system and storage devices.
  • Function:
    • Allocates storage space for files in blocks.
    • Abstracts hardware differences for operating systems.
    • Example:
      • A 4 TB HDD has ~1 billion blocks, each 4,096 bytes.
      • LBA manages these blocks and assigns them to files as needed.
  • Key Points:
    • No need to manually install drivers for most storage devices.
    • Ensure the BIOS recognizes the storage device, and it will work automatically with the OS.

3. Capacity and Counting Systems

  • Two systems for measuring storage:
    • Decimal (Base-10):
      • Common for manufacturers.
      • 1,000 bytes = 1 kilobyte (kilo), 1,000 KB = 1 MB (mega), etc.
    • Binary (Base-2):
      • Used by computers.
      • 1,024 bytes = 1 kibibyte (kibi), 1,024 KiB = 1 MiB (mebi), etc.
    • Examples:
      • 1 MB = 1,000,000 bytes.
      • 1 MiB = 1,048,576 bytes (2^20).
  • Resulting Issue:
    • Discrepancy between reported storage capacity (decimal) vs. usable storage (binary).

4. Physical Storage Device Sizes

  • 5.25-inch: Used mainly for optical media.
  • 3.5-inch: Dominant for HDDs, common for large-capacity drives.
  • 2.5-inch: Originally for laptops; now popular for servers and other compact systems.
  • 1.8-inch: Rare; mainly SSDs (supplanted by M.2).
  • M.2: Current standard for SSDs; dominant format today.

5. Key Takeaways

  1. LBA makes storage device management seamless across operating systems. No additional drivers needed in most cases.
  1. Capacity Differences are due to binary vs. decimal counting; it's not lost storage but a counting discrepancy.
  1. Physical Sizes of storage devices vary; understanding the formats is important as they appear across various systems.

Magnetic Disk Drives

Basic Components

  • Platters:
    • Shiny, circular discs storing data using magnetic fields.
    • Store binary data (ones and zeros) via magnetism at a microscopic level.
  • Read-Write Arm:
    • Moves back and forth near the platters, without touching them, to read or write data.
    • Uses a read-write head to interact with the magnetized sectors on the platters.
  • Sectors:
    • The smallest storage unit on a platter.

Evolution and Sizes

  • HDDs have evolved in shape, size, and capacity over decades.
  • Physical sizes:
    • 3.5-inch and 2.5-inch drives are the most common sizes today.
  • Capacities:
    • Early drives (1990s): ~20 MB.
    • Modern drives: Up to 16 TB or more.

Communication Standards

  • ATA (Advanced Technology Attachment): The language used to communicate with HDDs.
    • Parallel ATA (PATA): Older standard; now obsolete.
    • Serial ATA (SATA): Current standard for HDD communication.
      • SATA Connectors:
        • Power connector (larger) with an "L" key.
        • Data connector (smaller) also with an "L" key.

Installation and Connectivity

  1. Power Connection:
    • Plug the SATA power connector into the drive.
  1. Data Connection:
    • Use a SATA cable to connect the HDD to the motherboard.
    • Motherboards typically have multiple SATA ports (often color-coded).
  1. External Devices:
    • eSATA: External version of SATA for connecting external HDDs.
      • Less popular today due to advances in USB technology.

Verification and Setup

  • After connecting the drive:
    • Boot the computer into system setup/BIOS.
    • Verify the HDD is detected (e.g., listed under boot options or storage).
    • Proceed to configure the drive within the operating system.

HDD Speed (RPM)

  • Classified by Revolutions Per Minute (RPM):
    • Common speeds: 5,400 RPM, 7,200 RPM, 10,000 RPM, and 15,000 RPM.
    • Higher RPM = faster data retrieval but may generate more heat and noise.
  • Moving parts include:
    • Spinning platters.
    • Read arm seeking data.

Solid State Drives (SSDs)

Most popular form of mass storage

Overview of SSDs

  • Solid-State Drives (SSDs):
    • Most popular mass storage type in modern systems.
    • Faster than Hard Disk Drives (HDDs); often used as the boot drive.
    • Systems can have both SSDs and HDDs simultaneously.
  • Structure:
    • SSDs are organized into pages, which contain blocks.
    • Each block is assigned an LBA (Logical Block Addressing) number, making them interchangeable in systems.

Types of SSDs

  1. 2.5-Inch SSDs:
    • Standard size with a case that connects using SATA (Serial ATA) interfaces for power and data.
  1. M.2 SSDs:
    • Small, stick-like drives in various lengths.
    • Two types based on interface:
      • SATA-based M.2:
        • Uses traditional SATA connection, compatible with older systems.
      • NVMe-based M.2:
        • Uses the NVMe (Non-Volatile Memory Express) interface.
        • Much faster, bypasses traditional hard drive controllers by allowing the CPU to handle storage directly.

Interfaces

  • SATA:
    • Maximum speed: 6 Gbps.
    • Used for both HDDs and first-gen SSDs.
    • Simple plug-and-play with existing systems.
  • NVMe:
    • Designed for speed; connects directly to the motherboard via M.2 slots.
    • Requires compatible motherboard connectors.
    • Not interchangeable with SATA M.2 slots.

Identifying M.2 Types

  • Physical Difference:
    • SATA M.2 has two notches.
    • NVMe M.2 has one notch.
  • Compatibility:
    • Check motherboard specs for M.2 slot type.
    • NVMe preferred for performance if supported.

Installing SSDs

  1. Installing NVMe M.2 SSD:
    • Locate M.2 slot on the motherboard (often under a heat sink).
    • Ensure the slot matches the notch type of the SSD.
    • Insert the SSD and secure it with a screw.
    • Replace the heat sink if applicable.
    • Confirm detection in system setup (BIOS/UEFI).
  1. Installing SATA SSD:
    • Connect the SATA data cable to the drive and the motherboard.
    • Connect the power cable from the power supply.
    • Ensure detection in system setup.

System Setup and Configuration

  • Check system setup (BIOS/UEFI) to confirm the drive is detected.
    • Drives appear under the Boot menu with their model numbers.
    • M.2 drives may be labeled (e.g., M1, M2) based on the motherboard slot.
  • After physical installation:
    • Boot into an operating system (Windows, Linux, macOS).
    • Configure the drives for use within the OS.

Key Tips

  • Always verify the motherboard's M.2 compatibility (SATA vs. NVMe) before purchase.
  • NVMe drives offer better performance but require the correct interface.
  • Read the motherboard manual and SSD packaging to ensure proper installation

Notes on RAID Transcript

Introduction to RAID:

  • RAID (Redundant Array of Independent Disks) was developed at Berkeley to address cost and performance issues with early disk drives.
  • RAID combines multiple drives to achieve speed and data redundancy.
  • Defined by "RAID levels" that dictate how drives work together.

Popular RAID Levels:

  1. RAID 0 (Striping):
    • Requires a minimum of 2 drives.
    • Data is split across drives (e.g., alternate segments of a file are stored on different drives).
    • Advantage: Increases speed as multiple drives write simultaneously.
    • Disadvantage: No redundancy—failure of one drive results in total data loss.
  1. RAID 1 (Mirroring):
    • Requires 2 drives (or more, typically in pairs).
    • Identical copies of data are written to each drive.
    • Advantage: Provides data redundancy.
    • Disadvantage: Slower write speeds due to duplication.
  1. RAID 5 (Striping with Parity):
    • Requires a minimum of 3 drives.
    • Data is striped across drives, with parity data (error-checking info) calculated and stored.
    • Advantage: Balances speed and redundancy; can survive the loss of 1 drive.
    • Disadvantage: Losing more than 1 drive results in total data loss.
  1. RAID 6:
    • Requires a minimum of 4 drives.
    • Similar to RAID 5, but calculates and stores two parity values.
    • Advantage: Can survive the failure of 2 drives.
    • Disadvantage: More storage space is used for parity.

Nested RAID Levels:

  • Combines features of multiple RAID types:
    1. RAID 10 (1+0):
      • Requires at least 4 drives (2 mirrored pairs).
      • Data is mirrored within each pair and striped across pairs.
      • Advantage: Survives failure of one drive per mirrored pair.
      • Disadvantage: If both drives in a mirrored pair fail, data is lost.
    1. RAID 0+1:
      • Also requires 4 drives.
      • Stripes data across pairs, then mirrors the stripes.
      • Advantage: Survives loss of one striped pair.
      • Disadvantage: Failure of one drive in each pair results in data loss.

RAID Implementation:

  1. Hardware RAID:
    • Uses dedicated RAID controllers (built-in motherboard or add-on cards).
    • Configuration is done in system setup/BIOS.
    • Drives appear as a single logical unit to the OS.
  1. Software RAID:
    • Managed through the operating system.
    • Offers flexibility but may use more system resources.

Proprietary RAID Systems:

  • Developed by companies like Synology or Microsoft (e.g., Storage Spaces).
  • Custom RAID solutions offering efficiency and unique features.
  • Details often not disclosed.

Hardware RAID

Notes on Hardware RAID Setup and Features

What is Hardware RAID?

  • Hardware RAID involves a high-end controller that configures hard drives into RAID arrays (e.g., RAID 0, RAID 1, RAID 5, RAID 10).
  • Once configured, the RAID array appears as a single drive to the operating system.

Modern Hardware RAID Options

  • Many desktop motherboards (over 50%) come with built-in RAID controllers.
  • These controllers are soldered to the motherboard and allow for RAID configuration without needing a separate high-end controller.

RAID Setup Steps

  1. System Preparation:
    • Boot from an M.2 drive (or another dedicated boot drive) with the operating system already installed.
    • Install identical hard drives for the RAID array (e.g., four 4TB drives in this example).
  1. Change Mode to RAID:
    • Access the motherboard's BIOS/system setup.
    • Change the drive configuration from AHCI mode (standard hard drive mode) to RAID mode.
    • This action dedicates the selected drives to the RAID array.
  1. RAID Configuration:
    • On reboot, a new RAID configuration utility appears (e.g., triggered with "Ctrl + R").
    • Select drives to include in the array.
    • Choose the RAID type (RAID 0, RAID 1, RAID 10, etc.).
    • Confirm the setup and initialize the array.
  1. Operating System Recognition:
    • Boot into the operating system (e.g., Windows).
    • Access Disk Management to initialize the new array.
    • Choose MBR or GPT formatting for the array. (GPT is recommended for modern systems.)

RAID Features

  • Hot Spare:
    • A hot spare is a dedicated backup drive that automatically activates to replace a failed drive in the array.
    • After activation, the RAID array rebuilds itself using the hot spare.
  • Hot Swappable Drives:
    • Allows for removing and replacing drives without shutting down the system.
    • Requires advanced RAID controllers; basic motherboard RAID controllers typically lack this capability.

Limitations of Built-in RAID Controllers

  • Some RAID features (e.g., RAID 5) may be unavailable without licensing or additional hardware.
  • Advanced controllers (costing ~$300–$400) provide:
    • Enhanced features (e.g., hot swap support).
    • Fewer licensing limitations.
    • Greater reliability and control.

RAID Controller Insights

  • The operating system views the RAID array as a single logical drive.
  • RAID controllers handle all array-level configuration and maintenance, abstracting complexities from the OS.

Software RAID

Hardware RAID vs. Software RAID

  • Hardware RAID:
    • Requires a dedicated RAID controller.
    • Faster performance as it offloads processing from the CPU.
    • More expensive.
    • Ideal for systems with higher budgets and performance needs.
  • Software RAID:
    • Managed by the operating system (OS).
    • Relies on CPU for RAID duties.
    • Slower performance compared to hardware RAID.
    • Free and flexible but can slow down systems with weaker CPUs.
    • Supported by Windows, Linux, and macOS.

RAID in Windows Disk Management

  • Windows supports RAID 0 (striped) and RAID 1 (mirrored) natively.
    • RAID 0:
      • Combines two or more drives for performance.
      • Requires at least two drives.
      • No redundancy (data loss if one drive fails).
    • RAID 1:
      • Mirrors data across two drives for redundancy.
      • Requires at least two drives.
      • Converts drives to dynamic disks for RAID setup.
  • Limited RAID support: RAID 5 requires advanced versions of Windows.

Windows Storage Spaces

  • Advanced software RAID tool included in most Windows versions.
  • Provides greater flexibility compared to Disk Management.
  • Allows creation of storage pools and custom RAID configurations.

Key RAID options in Storage Spaces:

  1. Simple (JBOD):
    • Combines drives without redundancy.
  1. Two-way mirror:
    • Mirrors data across two drives for redundancy.
  1. Three-way mirror (Microsoft proprietary RAID):
    • Mirrors data across five drives.
    • Can handle failure of any two drives.
    • Efficiently uses drives of varying sizes.
    • Supports external drives.
  1. Parity (RAID 5):
    • Requires at least three drives.
    • Combines storage with redundancy using parity.
    • Equivalent storage of one drive is used for parity.

Three-way Mirror Features

  • Proprietary technology with undisclosed mechanisms.
  • Efficiently uses drives of different sizes without wastage.
  • Allows large, flexible volume sizes (e.g., 1 TB, even with smaller actual capacity).
  • Can use external drives and supports plug-and-play.

Performance Considerations

  • Hardware RAID:
    • Offers higher speed and reliability for mission-critical systems.
    • Ideal for setups where CPU performance is a priority.
  • Software RAID:
    • Cost-effective and integrated into OS.
    • May impact system performance on less powerful CPUs.

Summary:
Software RAID is an excellent low-cost, flexible solution for personal and small business use. However, for enterprise-level performance and reliability, hardware RAID remains the better choice. Storage Spaces adds powerful options for RAID configurations, especially with proprietary features like the three-way mirror.

Encrypting Mass Storage

Notes on Encryption Process

Importance of Encryption

  • Device portability risk: Portable devices like laptops are prone to being left behind, increasing the risk of exposing sensitive data.
  • Purpose: Encrypt storage devices to protect company secrets and prevent unauthorized access.

Types of Encryption

  1. File-Based Encryption:
    • Encrypts specific files or folders.
    • Examples:
      • Windows: Uses Encrypting File System (EFS) integrated with NTFS.
      • macOS: No built-in file encryption, but third-party tools are available.
      • Linux: Offers a variety of file-level encryption tools.
    • Process (Windows):
      • Format partition with NTFS.
      • Right-click file > Properties > Advanced > Check Encrypt contents to secure data.
      • File auto-decrypts for the logged-in user but stays encrypted otherwise.
  1. Disk-Based Encryption:
    • Encrypts entire drives or partitions.
    • Examples:
      • Windows: BitLocker.
      • macOS: File Vault.
      • Linux: Numerous options available.
    • Process:
      • Requires a Trusted Platform Module (TPM) for enhanced security.
      • Encryption key is stored in TPM, making it difficult to access without the original device.

Setting Up Encryption

  1. Enable TPM (if not already):
    • Access system setup (BIOS/UEFI).
    • Navigate to the Security tab > Turn on TPM under Trusted Computing.
    • Save changes and reboot.
  1. Using BitLocker (Windows):
    • Access from Control Panel.
    • Follow steps to enable:
      • Verify TPM is active.
      • Save recovery key to an external drive or cloud storage.
      • Choose to encrypt:
        • Entire drive or only used space.
        • Use new encryption mode (modern systems) or compatible mode (older systems).
      • Start encryption process.
  1. Using BitLocker to Go (Removable Media):
    • Works without TPM.
    • Requires a strong password for encryption.
    • Recovery key can be saved or printed for backup.

Key Points

  • File-Based vs. Disk-Based Encryption:
    • File-based is for individual files or folders.
    • Disk-based encrypts the entire drive for a higher level of security.
  • BitLocker to Go: Allows removable drives to be encrypted without TPM, using a password.

Benefits of Encryption

  • Data remains protected even if the device is lost or stolen.
  • Recovery options ensure encrypted data can be accessed when hardware fails.
  • Supports compliance with organizational security requirements.

  1. Mental reinstall - go through process again and again to make sure you go through each step
  1. Triple check

General Rules for Troubleshooting Mass Storage:

  1. Backup Data:
    • Always back up critical data before troubleshooting. Even a quick backup to a thumb drive is better than nothing.
    • Loss of data can be permanent or expensive to recover.
  1. Mental Reinstall:
    • Revisit the installation process mentally, especially for new devices.
    • Perform a triple check on connections and settings; human error is a common cause of issues.

Common Mass Storage Issues and Solutions:

  1. RAID Issues:
    • RAID Not Found:
      • Check if the RAID controller is active.
      • Ensure proper drivers are installed.
      • Verify physical connections and power to RAID drives.
    • RAID Stops Working:
      • Treat it like a single hard drive issue.
      • Check connections, power, and ensure no accidental data deletion.
  1. Read/Write Failures:
    • Often due to the drive reaching the end of its lifespan.
    • Use S.M.A.R.T. (Self-Monitoring, Analysis, and Reporting Technology) tools to assess drive health.
    • Ultimately, the solution is to replace the failing drive.
  1. Slow Performance:
    • Likely caused by insufficient system RAM, leading to heavy use of virtual memory and disk thrashing.
    • Solution: Add more RAM to the system.
  1. Loud Clicking Noise (Click of Death):
    • Indicates physical failure of the drive.
    • Backup data immediately (if possible) and replace the drive.
  1. Failure to Boot:
    • Check the boot order in BIOS/UEFI settings.
    • Use Windows Recovery Environment (WinRE) tools to resolve boot issues.
  1. Drive Not Recognized:
    • Likely caused by formatting or initialization issues.
    • Reformat the drive if no critical data exists.
    • For drives moved between systems, ensure they are initialized in the new system.
  1. OS Not Found:
    • Usually caused by an incorrect boot order.
    • Inspect system setup for any overlooked boot devices (e.g., hidden thumb drives).
  1. Attempts to Boot to Incorrect Device:
    • Caused by boot order misconfiguration.
    • Correct the boot order in BIOS/UEFI.
  1. Continuous Reboots:
    • Rarely related to mass storage; often due to operating system corruption.
    • Refer to specific troubleshooting methods for operating systems.

Other Considerations:

  • Controller Issues:
    • Ensure SATA, RAID, or USB controllers are enabled in system settings.
    • Controllers may be disabled for security reasons or by accident.
  • Exam Tips:
    • CompTIA troubleshooting scenarios often hide the key question in a verbose description. Read carefully.
    • Boot order problems are common and can be a frequent source of exam and real-world issues.

Understanding USB

Introduction to USB

  • USB simplifies connectivity: plug-and-play functionality.

Upstream and Downstream Concepts

  • Downstream: A connectors (e.g., standard rectangular plugs).

USB Connector Types

  • B Connector: Found on devices; for upstream connections.

USB Controllers and Root Hubs

  • Motherboards contain USB controllers connected to root hubs.
  • Controllers support various USB versions (e.g., 1.1, 3.1).
    • Ports are color-coded for identification (e.g., blue for USB 3.1).
  • USB hubs expand connectivity (up to 127 devices).
    • Powered hubs: Include AC connectors for external power to support multiple devices.

Device Management

  • Use the operating system (e.g., Windows Device Manager) to view:
    • Host controllers.
    • Root hubs and connected devices.
  • USB devices often self-configure on modern operating systems.

Key Points

  • A and B connectors distinguish device roles.
  • USB hubs extend device connectivity but may require external power.

Thunderbolt and Lightning Connections

Thunderbolt

  • Overview:
    • Combines PCIe and DisplayPort into a single versatile connection.
    • Supports monitors, charging, data transfer, and more.
    • Extremely high throughput; capable of driving up to six monitors.
  • Versions:
    • Version 1: Two-channel setup, total throughput of 20 Gbps.

    • Version 3: 40 Gbps, utilizes USB-C connectors.
  • Connectors:
    • Versions 1 & 2: Use Mini DisplayPort connectors.
    • Version 3: Uses USB-C, but Thunderbolt 3 and USB-C are different standards.
      • USB-C focuses on the connector type.
      • Thunderbolt 3 ensures compatibility with USB-C devices (e.g., USB-C mouse).
  • Identification:
    • Thunderbolt ports are often marked with a small lightning bolt symbol.
  • Uses:
    • Charging devices, connecting peripherals, and high-speed data transfer.
    • Offers high compatibility with USB-C devices, which often work when plugged into Thunderbolt ports.

Lightning

  • Overview:
    • Proprietary connection exclusive to Apple products.
    • Used for charging and data transfer.
    • Commonly found on iOS devices.
  • Devices:
    • Charges wireless peripherals like keyboards, mice, and earbuds.
    • Compatible with most Apple smartphones and tablets.
  • Mac vs. iOS:
    • macOS devices: Typically use Thunderbolt (often via USB-C).
    • iOS devices: Use Lightning connectors.

Thunderbolt vs. Lightning

  • Key Differences:
    • Thunderbolt: Versatile, high-speed, supports multiple platforms (e.g., macOS, Windows, and Android with USB-C).
    • Lightning: Exclusive to Apple and primarily focused on charging and data transfer for iOS.
  • Compatibility:
    • USB-C devices can often interoperate with Thunderbolt connections.
    • Thunderbolt adoption in macOS products is increasing, often using USB-C connectors.

Installing and troubleshooting expansion cards

Overview of Expansion Cards

  • Purpose: Add functionality to PCs, such as improved graphics, sound, or network capabilities.
  • Importance: Even in a world of built-to-spec PCs, technicians often need to install or troubleshoot expansion cards.

PCI and PCI Express (PCIe)

  1. PCI (Peripheral Component Interconnect):
    • An older plug-and-play standard.
    • Key innovation: Eliminated the need for jumpers or switches.
  1. PCI Express (PCIe):
    • Successor to PCI; not backward compatible.
    • Features:
      • High-bandwidth communication between the card and motherboard.
      • Supports a variety of expansion cards, including GPUs, sound cards, network interface cards, and RAID cards.

PCIe Slot Formats

  • Slots are designed with varying lane configurations:
    • x1, x4, x8, x16 (e.g., x16 has more bandwidth than x1).
    • Analogy: Lanes on a highway – more lanes = more traffic capacity.
  • Motherboards typically include a mix of x1 and x16 slots.
    • GPUs are installed in x16 slots for maximum performance.

Installing a GPU in a PCIe Slot

  1. Preparation:
    • Identify the primary PCIe x16 slot for the GPU.
    • Note the lever mechanism on the slot for securing and removing the card.
  1. Installation Steps:
    • Align the GPU with the slot and slide it in.
    • Listen for a click to confirm a secure connection.
    • Ensure the GPU is powered:
      • Plug in the appropriate power connectors from the PSU.

Troubleshooting a GPU Installation

  1. Common Issues:
    • No video signal despite installation.
    • Causes:
      • GPU not seated properly.
      • Power connectors not plugged in.
      • Onboard video not disabled.
  1. CompTIA Troubleshooting Steps:
    • Verify connections:
      • Reseat the GPU.
      • Confirm both PSU power leads are plugged into the GPU.
    • Check for onboard video conflicts:
      • If the system uses integrated graphics (onboard video or an APU), disable it in the BIOS.
    • Test power:
      • Ensure the PSU is functional and supplying power to all components.
  1. Resolution Example:
    • Problem: Forgot to connect the GPU's power leads.
    • Solution:
      • Unplug PSU.
      • Connect power leads to the modular PSU.
      • Reapply power, ensure the PSU is on, and restart the system.
    • Result: Video signal restored.

Key Considerations

  • Expansion cards require proper seating, power, and configuration to function.
  • BIOS configuration may be necessary when switching from onboard video to a dedicated GPU.
  • Familiarity with PCIe slot types and troubleshooting techniques is essential for PC technicians.
