Legal Hold - a legal technique to preserve relevant information

Digital Forensics & Evidence Collection

  • Security professionals collect data during security events for analysis, future protection, and legal proceedings.
  • RFC 3227 provides guidelines for evidence collection and archiving.
  • Best practices exist for data acquisition, analysis, and reporting, ensuring integrity and legal admissibility.

Legal Holds & Data Preservation

  • Legal hold: A request from legal entities to preserve specific data.
  • Data custodian: Responsible for handling and storing legally held data.
  • Storage considerations:
    • Data might need format conversion (e.g., emails from proprietary to standard formats).
    • Data must be preserved securely for potential court use.

Chain of Custody & Data Integrity

  • Ensures data remains unmodified from acquisition to legal use.
  • Digital forensics uses hashing and digital signatures to track access and maintain integrity.
  • Required for all collected data, including logs, files, and memory captures.

Data Sources for Forensics

  • Multiple sources may require data collection:
    • Storage: Hard drives, memory, firmware, file systems.
    • Network: Logs from firewalls, servers, and network devices.
    • Virtual systems: Snapshots of virtual machines.
    • Hidden data: Logs, temporary files, saved browser data, recycle bin contents.

Documentation & Reporting

  • Acquisition process must be documented in detail for internal and legal use.
  • Reports include:
    • Event summary: Overview of why data was acquired.
    • Step-by-step acquisition details: Ensures reproducibility and integrity.
    • Data analysis: Structure and usability of collected data.
    • Conclusions: Insights into the security event.

Data Storage & Live Collection

  • Preservation of data is critical, especially for long-term legal cases.
  • Working from copies prevents modifications to the original data.
  • Live data collection:
    • Crucial for systems with encryption that locks on shutdown.
    • Used for acquiring volatile data (e.g., running processes, network connections).

E-Discovery vs. Forensics

  • E-Discovery: Collection of digital evidence without analysis.
  • Forensics: Investigation and interpretation of data to determine security events.
  • Example:
    • E-Discovery: Creating an image of a drive.
    • Forensics: Analyzing the drive for deleted files and evidence.

Key Takeaways

  • Digital forensics requires strict procedures for data integrity and legal use.
  • Best practices must be followed in acquisition, storage, and reporting.
  • Security professionals play a vital role in evidence preservation and forensic readiness.
